## The Pattern I Kept Seeing 

I've been building digital products since 2002. Agencies. Mailigen, which I co-founded and grew to a few million. Pipedrive as VP Product, watching teams burn entire quarters on prototypes nobody used.

The pattern was always the same: great idea → 3 months of development → user feedback: "actually, we need something different." $50,000 and a quarter gone. Every single time.

Nobody questioned the process. That's just how software gets built.

Then something shifted.

---

## What Actually Changed at Aerones

At the end of 2025 we had an idea for a customer portal. Old me would've written a requirements doc, passed it on to the team, put it in the backlog, and waited for dev bandwidth.

Instead: idea on Wednesday afternoon, working demo in front of actual customers by Friday morning. They clicked around, told us what sucked, we fixed it over lunch.

I built it, and I'm not a developer — a product leader who'd been using AI building tools for a few months.

The math hit me: we'd just run a full validation cycle in 18 hours that would've taken 6 weeks. Not because I'm clever. Because the tool did what used to require a developer.

And here's the thing — the customers didn't care who built it. They cared that we showed up with something real.

## The Leadership Shift Nobody's Talking About

Most articles about AI coding tools focus on speed. "Build an MVP in hours!" That's real, but it's not the interesting part.

The interesting part is what happens to your team.

Your PM has product instincts sharpened by daily customer conversations. Your designer sees UX problems nobody else notices. Your customer success lead knows exactly which features would cut support tickets in half. They've always had these insights. They just couldn't act on them without a developer.

Now they can.

That backlog feature my CTO friend's PM shipped in a weekend? It had been prioritized, deprioritized, re-scoped, and argued over for six months. Six months of meetings. One weekend of building.

This isn't about replacing developers. The best ones on my team now spend their time on the 20% that actually needs serious engineering — the hard infrastructure, the algorithmic stuff, the things that break at scale. That's a better use of them than writing authentication flows for the 500th time.

## The Question Worth Asking

Look at your backlog. Find the feature that's been there for 3+ months — the one everyone agrees is a good idea but never gets to the top of the dev queue.

Who on your team has the deepest understanding of why that feature matters?

That person probably isn't a developer.

Give them a Lovable account and a weekend. See what happens.

My daughter is 8. She asked me last month if she could make an app for her school friends. We built it on a Sunday morning — took about an hour. She was more proud of that than anything I've shipped at work in years.

That gap between idea and reality is closing for everyone. The leaders who figure out how to use that — who unlock their whole team, not just their dev team — are going to move faster than everyone else.

The ones who don't will keep running the same expensive, slow cycle I ran for 20 years.
